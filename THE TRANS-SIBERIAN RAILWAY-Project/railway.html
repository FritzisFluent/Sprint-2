The Trans-Siberian Railway: the world's longest railroad journey

Vladivostok, Khabarovsk, Izhevsk, Moscow — 18 days en route in total. This is how musician David Bowie traveled to Europe after his tour of Japan in the spring of 1973. Bowie had a fear of flying, so he took the train.

First, you see the familiar landscapes of the European part of Russia: fields, cottages, villages, woods, and power lines. It feels almost like travelling through the land of the hobbits, or into the past, back to 1973; if feels like David Bowie could walk in from the next compartment at any moment wearing a Japanese kimono and bright pants.

It's time to get into your element. In Kostroma, you get off the train and buy all the things you forgot to pack: instant coffee, instant noodles, and a six pack of beer. Now make yourself comfortable. We're not even halfway there yet.

Your journey has transcended the physical realm and moved into the spiritual. This mystical experince of immersing yourself in the depths of Russia's soul will stay with you forever. Europe is far behind, and Asia is still lightyears away. You may even have been stationary this whole time. You've lost the count of how many miles you've traveled, and now you're not even sure if train is moving at all. On the other hand, if you look out the window for a long time (to be honest, there's not much else to do), you can see ancient mammoths of past millenia wandering these plains.

You're now at the very heart of the continent, and there's no sign of either ancient civilizations, or of Europe and Asia. Here the train passes through the highest point of the Trans-Siberian Railway, and the path to the sea begins. Before, the road narrowed here to one track and went through a tiny tunnel, the entrance to which was adorned with a sign pointing you towards the great ocean.

You're starting to think you'll most likely spend the rest of your life in the train when Khabarovsk appears on the horizon. You already know more about your fellow passengers than about your family members. Anyway, only some 800 kilometers more to go, and you'll be arriving in Vladivostok.

Vladivostok

This has been an extraordinary journey. Everyone has their very own story to tell, but anyone who undertakes this journey is a different person to the one who started it.
